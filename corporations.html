THE CONFLICT OF INTEREST

The ISA is both the regulator and the promoter.

The International Seabed Authority's mandate under UNCLOS requires it to both regulate deep sea mining and promote the development of ocean mineral resources. Critics argue this creates a fundamental conflict of interest.

The ISA is partly funded by the contractors it regulates. Its council — which decides on mining rules — includes representation from countries with active mining interests. And it has repeatedly delayed finalizing binding environmental regulations while continuing to issue exploration contracts.

Germany holds an ISA exploration contract in the CCZ while simultaneously calling for a moratorium on mining. France does too. This is not a contradiction unique to one country — it reflects how the system is designed.
