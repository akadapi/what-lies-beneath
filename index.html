THE BASICS

What is deep sea mining?

Deep sea mining is the extraction of mineral deposits — called polymetallic nodules — from the ocean floor, typically at depths of 1,400 to 3,700 metres. These potato-sized lumps are rich in cobalt, nickel, manganese, and copper.

Mining companies argue these minerals are needed to build EV batteries and renewable energy infrastructure. Critics argue we're destroying ecosystems we barely understand to fuel a consumption problem we should be solving differently.

The Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) — a stretch of Pacific Ocean between Hawaii and Mexico the size of the continental United States — is the primary target. Over 5,000 species entirely new to science have been discovered there.

HOW WE GOT HERE

A timeline of decisions

2001
ISA issues first contracts

The International Seabed Authority begins issuing exploration contracts for the CCZ to state-sponsored companies.

2021
Nauru triggers the "two-year rule"

The tiny Pacific island nation — sponsored by The Metals Company — triggers a legal clause forcing the ISA to finalize mining regulations within two years or allow mining under draft rules.

2023
ISA misses its own deadline

The ISA fails to publish final commercial mining regulations by July 2023. The deadline is pushed to 2025. Mining companies can still apply for licenses.

2024
32 nations call for a moratorium

Countries including Germany, Chile, France, New Zealand, and 26 others formally call for a pause on deep sea mining. The ISA does not comply.

Apr 2025
Trump signs executive order fast-tracking US mining

President Trump signs an executive order directing NOAA to accelerate deep sea mining permits for US companies — bypassing the international ISA framework entirely.

2025
The Metals Company applies to NOAA

TMC formally applies to NOAA for commercial mining rights in the CCZ — one week after Trump's executive order — seeking to mine outside ISA jurisdiction.

Sources: ISA (isa.org.jm); CSIS Asia Maritime Transparency Initiative, May 2025; Congress.gov CRS Report IG10053, Feb 2026
WHY IT MATTERS

The ocean isn't empty down there.

🌊

Carbon Sink

The deep ocean absorbs roughly 30% of all CO₂ produced by humans. Disrupting the seafloor releases stored carbon, accelerating climate change.

Source: IPCC Ocean & Cryosphere Report
🐙

Unknown Life

Scientists estimate 88–92% of species in the CCZ have never been described. We are permanently destroying life we haven't even discovered yet.

Source: Frontiers in Marine Science, 2024
🌍

No Recovery

Sediment plumes from mining travel over 1,000km from extraction sites. Studies show disturbed areas show little recovery even decades later.

Source: Nature Scientific Reports
