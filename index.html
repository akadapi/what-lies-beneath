The Basics

What is deep sea mining?

Deep sea mining is the extraction of mineral deposits from the ocean floor, typically at depths of 1,400 to 3,700 metres. The primary targets are polymetallic nodules — potato-sized rocks rich in cobalt, nickel, manganese, and copper.

Mining companies argue these minerals are needed for EV batteries and renewable energy. Critics argue we're destroying ecosystems we barely understand to fuel a consumption problem we should solve differently.

The Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) — a stretch of the Pacific the size of the continental United States — is the main target. Over 5,000 species entirely new to science have been found there.

How We Got Here

A timeline of decisions

2001
ISA issues first exploration contracts

The International Seabed Authority begins issuing exploration contracts for the CCZ to state-sponsored companies.

2021
Nauru triggers the "two-year rule"

The tiny Pacific island nation — sponsored by The Metals Company — triggers a legal clause forcing the ISA to finalize mining regulations within two years, or allow mining under draft rules.

2023
ISA misses its own deadline

The ISA fails to publish final commercial mining regulations by July 2023. The deadline is pushed. Mining companies can still apply for licenses.

2024
32 nations call for a moratorium

Germany, Chile, France, New Zealand, and 28 others formally call for a pause on deep sea mining. The ISA does not comply.

Apr 2025
Trump signs executive order fast-tracking US mining

President Trump directs NOAA to accelerate deep sea mining permits — bypassing the international ISA framework entirely.

2025
The Metals Company applies to NOAA

TMC formally applies to NOAA for commercial mining rights in the CCZ — one week after Trump's executive order — seeking to mine outside ISA jurisdiction.

Why It Matters

The ocean isn't empty down there.

🌊

Carbon Sink

The deep ocean absorbs roughly 30% of all CO₂ produced by humans. Disrupting the seafloor releases stored carbon, accelerating climate change.

Source: IPCC Ocean & Cryosphere Report
🐙

Unknown Life

Scientists estimate 88–92% of CCZ species have never been described. We are permanently destroying life we haven't even discovered yet.

Source: Frontiers in Marine Science, 2024
🌍

No Recovery

Sediment plumes travel over 1,000km from extraction sites. Disturbed areas show little to no recovery even decades later.

Source: Nature Scientific Reports
