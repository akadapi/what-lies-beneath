The Science

Two problems the industry downplays

The sediment plume problem

When a mining vehicle vacuums nodules from the seafloor, it generates an enormous cloud of sediment. This plume spreads through the water column, smothering filter-feeding organisms that cannot move.

A 2021 test by Belgian company GSR using their Patania II vehicle generated a plume that traveled well beyond the licensed mining zone. Studies of 1970s disturbance sites show minimal recovery 30–50 years later.

Sources: Frontiers in Marine Science, 2024 (GSR Patania II trial study) · Deep Sea Conservation Coalition

The carbon sink at risk

The deep ocean locks carbon into seafloor sediments for thousands of years. Organic matter sinks from the surface, is processed by deep-sea organisms, and stays put — until it's disturbed.

Mining resuspends sediment and destroys the biological pump that moves carbon downward. The IPCC has flagged deep sea mining as a potential threat to ocean carbon cycling — yet the ISA has not made full environmental impact assessment mandatory before licensing.

Source: IPCC Ocean & Cryosphere Report · ISA environmental policy framework
Species at Stake

Life with nowhere else to go

These are real species found in the Clarion-Clipperton Zone. Many were discovered in the last 20 years. None have a backup habitat.

🦑

Vampire Squid

A living fossil unchanged for 300 million years. Survived every mass extinction — but has no way to escape a sediment plume.

Source: MBARI
🐙

Deep-sea Octopus

Several undescribed species lay their eggs directly on polymetallic nodules. Mining destroys both the nodules and the eggs — permanently.

Source: Schmidt Ocean Institute
🦀

Yeti Crab

First discovered in 2005. Entirely dependent on hydrothermal vent ecosystems. Critically threatened by sulphide mining on ocean ridges.

Source: MBARI
🐠

Bristlemouth Fish

The most numerous vertebrate on Earth by total count — yet almost entirely unknown. Mining disrupts their food chain from the bottom up.

Source: NOAA
🦈

Frilled Shark

A living relic from 80 million years ago. Its deep-water habitat overlaps directly with multiple ISA contract zones.

Source: IUCN Red List
🔬

~5,000 unnamed species

In the CCZ alone, over 5,000 species new to science have been found. We don't know what most of them do or how the ecosystem depends on them.

Source: WRI 2024 · Frontiers in Marine Science 2024
