The sediment plume problem

When a mining vehicle vacuums nodules from the seafloor, it kicks up an enormous cloud of fine sediment. This cloud — called a plume — spreads through the water column and across the ocean floor.

A 2021 test by Belgian company GSR using their Patania II vehicle generated a plume that traveled well beyond the mining zone, smothering filter-feeding organisms that cannot move out of the way.

Studies of historical disturbance sites from the 1970s and 80s show that 30–50 years later, recovery is minimal. The organisms that live here reproduce slowly, grow slowly, and have nowhere else to go.

Sources: Frontiers in Marine Science, 2024 (GSR Patania II trial study); Deep Sea Conservation Coalition

The carbon sink at risk

The deep ocean is one of Earth's most critical systems for managing carbon. Organic matter sinks from the surface, is consumed by deep-sea organisms, and gets locked into seafloor sediments for thousands of years.

Mining disturbs this system in ways scientists are still trying to measure. Sediment resuspension can release stored carbon back into the water column. Destroying filter feeders and sediment-dwelling organisms disrupts the biological pump that moves carbon to the deep.

The IPCC has flagged deep sea mining as a potential threat to ocean carbon cycling — but the ISA has not made environmental impact assessment mandatory before licensing.

Source: IPCC Ocean & Cryosphere Report; ISA environmental policy framework

Species with everything at stake

These are real species found in the Clarion-Clipperton Zone. Many were discovered in the last 20 years. None have a backup habitat.

🦑

Vampire Squid

A living fossil unchanged for 300 million years. It survived every mass extinction — but has no mechanism to escape a sediment plume.

Found throughout CCZ depths · Source: MBARI
🐙

Deep-sea Octopus

Several undescribed species lay their eggs directly on polymetallic nodules. Mining destroys both the nodules and the eggs — permanently.

CCZ species · Source: Schmidt Ocean Institute
🦀

Yeti Crab

First discovered in 2005. Entirely dependent on hydrothermal vent ecosystems. Critically threatened by sulphide mining on ocean ridges.

Mid-Ocean Ridge species · Source: MBARI
🐠

Bristlemouth Fish

The most numerous vertebrate on Earth by count — yet almost entirely unknown. Sediment disruption affects their food chain from the bottom up.

Deep mesopelagic zone · Source: NOAA
🦈

Frilled Shark

A living relic from 80 million years ago — older than the dinosaurs. Its deep-water habitat overlaps directly with multiple ISA contract zones.

NW Pacific deep water · Source: IUCN Red List
🔬

~5,000 unnamed species

In the CCZ alone, over 5,000 species new to science have been found. We don't know what most of them do, what they eat, or how the ecosystem depends on them.

Source: World Resources Institute 2024; Frontiers in Marine Science 2024
