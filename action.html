The Bigger Picture

Why this matters beyond the ocean

The argument for deep sea mining is that we need cobalt and nickel for EV batteries to power the green transition. But this framing deserves scrutiny.

Battery technology is rapidly evolving away from cobalt — toward lithium-iron-phosphate and sodium-ion chemistries that don't need seabed minerals. Mining the ocean for current technology could create permanent damage for a problem the market solves within a decade.

Meanwhile, land-based recycling of critical minerals is dramatically underfunded compared to new extraction. The EU estimates recycling could meet a significant share of demand by 2030 — without touching the seafloor.

The governance question is equally serious. The ISA was designed to treat the ocean as the "common heritage of humankind." In practice it has functioned as a permitting body for wealthy nations and corporations.

The 2025 US executive order bypassing the ISA entirely is a significant escalation. Portugal has already passed a law banning deep sea mining in its national waters for 25 years. Germany and Canada have called for moratoria. The science is not on the side of moving fast.
