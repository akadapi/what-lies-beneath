THE BIGGER PICTURE

Why this matters beyond the ocean

The argument for deep sea mining is that we need cobalt, nickel, and manganese to build EV batteries for the green energy transition. This framing deserves scrutiny.

Independent researchers point out that battery technology is rapidly evolving away from cobalt and toward lithium-iron-phosphate and sodium-ion chemistries — which don't require seabed minerals. Mining the ocean floor to fuel current battery technology may create a problem that the market solves on its own within a decade.

Meanwhile, existing land-based recycling of critical minerals is dramatically underfunded compared to new extraction. The EU estimates that recycling could meet a significant share of demand for these minerals by 2030 — without touching the ocean floor.

There's also the governance question. The ISA was designed to treat the ocean floor as the "common heritage of humankind" — resources belonging to all people, managed for collective benefit. In practice it has functioned as a permitting body for wealthy nations and multinational corporations.

The 2025 US executive order bypassing the ISA entirely represents a significant escalation — effectively saying the US will mine regardless of international consensus. This is the moment to pay attention.

Portugal has already passed a law banning deep sea mining in its national waters for 25 years. Germany and Canada have called for moratoria. The science is not on the side of moving fast.

Sources: CSIS AMTI May 2025; European Parliament; WRI 2024; Congress.gov CRS IG10053
